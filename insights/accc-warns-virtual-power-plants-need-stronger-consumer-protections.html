ACCC warns virtual power plants need stronger consumer protections

The Snapshot: A 107 per cent surge in home battery complaints is undermining trust in Australia's booming residential storage market, threatening uptake of virtual power plants that deliver the biggest bill savings and grid benefits. Consumer protection gaps around system sizing, switching rights and operator discretion are now material risks to the success of the federal battery scheme and grid modernisation.

More than 400,000 home batteries have been installed under Australia's federal rebate scheme, with virtual power plant customers seeing median bill reductions of $762-$1,093 compared to grid-only consumers. Yet complaints to ombudsman and fair trading offices surged 107 per cent in the first quarter of 2026. The issues span performance problems, misleading installer advice (including oversized system recommendations), deposit delays, and limited switching rights between virtual power plant operators.

Virtual power plants hold particular promise for grid stability and cost reduction. By linking multiple batteries to inject power during peak demand, they deliver greater network efficiencies and reduce spending on poles and wires infrastructure, benefiting all customers. However, participation lags behind battery and solar uptake. When customers join a virtual power plant, they cede operational control of their battery to an operator, bearing most of the risk while operators retain significant discretion over system use.

The ACCC has flagged critical protection gaps. Batteries compatible with only specific virtual power plant products can trap customers and prevent switching. There is no overarching duty on energy providers to ensure customers receive promised benefits. Customers struggle to compare offers transparently or resolve issues when things go wrong. Without these protections, the watchdog warns, eroding consumer trust could constrain the rollout of the technology that delivers the largest grid and bill benefits, undermining both the federal scheme's intent and network modernisation gains.

For businesses evaluating distributed energy procurement or virtual power plant participation, the message is clear: demand clarity on system compatibility, switching rights, operator obligations, and performance guarantees before committing capital. The regulatory landscape is tightening, and protections that may seem lacking today are likely to become mandatory, making them prudent safeguards for any procurement decision now.
