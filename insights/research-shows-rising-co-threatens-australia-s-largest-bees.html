Research shows rising CO₂ threatens Australia's largest bees

The Snapshot: Rising atmospheric CO₂ is shrinking populations of larger bee species while favouring smaller ones, threatening the pollination services that underpin Australian agriculture and ecosystems. This shifts focus from temperature alone to the chemical effects of emissions on pollinator biology, with clear implications for land management and agricultural resilience decisions.

Research examining pollinator responses across 25 sites shows larger bee genera—including Xylocopa and Amegilla species found in Australia—are less abundant in higher-CO₂ environments, while smaller bees like Ceratina and Lasioglossum thrive. The mechanism is biological: elevated CO₂ reduces protein in flower pollen and alters nectar composition, while potentially accelerating how quickly pollinators metabolise fats. Large bees already face pressure from climate change because they retain more heat and have higher metabolic demands, making them doubly vulnerable.

This matters for Australian business because larger bees are disproportionately effective pollinators. They carry and deposit more pollen than smaller species, travel longer distances, and are specifically matched to certain plants through evolution. Melastoma in Australia, for example, relies on large Xylocopa bees using vibrations to release pollen. Losing this pollination capacity directly threatens crop yields and wild plant reproduction across agricultural and natural systems.

Australia's role as the second-largest fossil fuel exporter means domestic emissions policy and land-use decisions directly shape CO₂ concentrations affecting these pollinator populations. The research points to four practical levers: habitat protection to prevent further land clearing, creation of wildlife corridors linking lower-CO₂ areas like dense forests, planting bee-friendly native trees (Eucalyptus, Banksia, Melaleuca and others), and reducing competing pressures on larger bee populations. For agricultural and land-management businesses, these aren't just conservation measures—they're infrastructure investments in pollination services that underpin productivity.
