Australia faces rising gas bills as customers abandon the network

The Snapshot: Australia's gas demand is falling faster than policy settings can manage, which risks either chaotic price spikes for remaining users or stranded infrastructure costs. Businesses relying on gas for heating, cooling or process heat need to plan transitions now, because government inaction will force compressed change later at higher cost.

Gas use in Australia is declining across households, electricity generation and industry. More homes leave the gas network than join it each year. Gas-fired power stations have backed fewer MW for a decade. Manufacturing is electrifying and shrinking its gas footprint. Yet governments have not matched policy to this trend, leaving the market to decline without direction. The result: a mismatch between falling demand and fixed infrastructure costs that will eventually push prices sharply higher for whoever stays on the network.

The core problem is a stranded asset trap. Gas pipeline networks are built for peak capacity. As demand falls, the costs of maintaining that network spread across fewer users, driving unit prices up. This creates a vicious cycle: higher prices accelerate departures; fewer users mean higher unit costs again. Without planned, managed transition, this compounds inequitably. Small businesses and households least able to afford switching face the steepest bills. Large industrial users with capital can exit earlier, leaving behind costlier networks for those who remain.

For businesses that use gas for space heating, hot water or industrial processes, the message is clear: waiting for government direction is a poor strategy. Planned transition to electrification or alternative fuels (where viable) lets you time the switch to your cash flow and operational needs, rather than react to price shocks. State-level policy on gas phase-out dates for heating and cooking is still patchy, and federal industrial rules remain loose. That policy vacuum will not last. When governments do act, they will likely act fast, and the least prepared businesses will bear the cost of rushed decisions.
