Study finds private jets emit heavy carbon at major events

The Snapshot: This analysis of private jet emissions at major sporting events highlights a policy gap relevant to Australian businesses: the absence of mechanisms to price or discourage high-emissions discretionary travel. For companies sponsoring or attending international events, it signals growing scrutiny of private aviation as a sustainability commitment, and suggests that carbon pricing or levies on private jets may become standard policy tools globally.

A recent study tracking private jet activity around three major 2023 sporting events in the US and Europe found striking spikes in emissions. Daily private jet operations more than doubled during the Super Bowl and increased eightfold around the Masters Tournament, generating an additional 33,000 tonnes of CO2-equivalent across the three events. The Monaco Grand Prix saw a 60% surge in private jet activity. These numbers matter because the research found that 81-90% of private jet routes to these events had direct commercial flight alternatives available, meaning most private aviation was driven by preference for luxury and convenience, not necessity. Had attendees flown commercially instead, private jet emissions could have been cut by up to 70%. The broader implication is policy-focused: the absence of carbon pricing or levies on private aviation leaves high-emissions discretionary travel essentially unpriced. That creates an incentive misalignment. As carbon accounting and scope 3 emissions reporting become standard for large Australian businesses, private jet use at corporate events, conferences and sponsorships will face increasing internal scrutiny. Several jurisdictions are now considering or implementing private jet levies or carbon pricing mechanisms. For Australian businesses with international travel commitments, this trend suggests that private aviation's true carbon cost will likely be reflected in policy within the next few years, making commercial flight alternatives more economically attractive by comparison.
