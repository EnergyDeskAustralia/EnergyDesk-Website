CSIRO warns gas costs rising as batteries reshape energy markets

The Snapshot: Global demand for gas turbines from US data centres is pushing up costs for gas-based generation worldwide, including in Australia. For businesses evaluating long-term energy procurement, this makes the case for renewable energy and storage stronger on cost grounds, while also signalling that gas will play only a limited role in Australia's future grid.

The CSIRO's GenCost 2025-26 report identifies a significant cost driver affecting gas technology across global markets: US data centres have become major consumers of gas turbines, sustaining upward pressure on equipment and installation costs. This matters for Australian businesses planning energy procurement because it affects the relative economics of different generation and peaking technologies available to the grid.

The report projects that electricity generation costs will continue falling in the near term as battery storage expands and becomes cheaper. Battery costs are already reshaping electricity markets by competing with traditional gas peaking generation, contributing to lower evening peak prices. This compression of the price advantage gas held during peak demand periods narrows the business case for gas-backed energy solutions in the short to medium term.

Looking to 2050, CSIRO modelling suggests gas and hydrogen will supply only three to seven per cent of Australian electricity generation, with solar and onshore wind providing 93 per cent. The report finds that a renewable energy and storage pathway delivers the lowest-cost route to net-zero electricity. For businesses making long-term energy decisions, this signals that gas investment now carries both cost and technology transition risk, while renewable procurement and storage partnerships are increasingly the foundation of least-cost, lowest-risk energy procurement.
