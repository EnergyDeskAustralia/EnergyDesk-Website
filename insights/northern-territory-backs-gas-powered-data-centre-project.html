Northern Territory backs gas-powered data centre project

The Snapshot: A major Australian data centre project is being planned around fracked gas rather than renewable energy, highlighting a tension between state-level energy policy and federal requirements for new data centres to match demand with firmed renewables. For businesses evaluating data centre procurement or energy supply partnerships, this signals that Australia's energy standards for large-scale infrastructure remain unsettled and that gas-based baseload remains competitive in some jurisdictions.

The Northern Territory government has granted exclusive land rights to develop a 2 gigawatt data centre powered primarily by Beetaloo Basin gas. The proposal would consume up to 200 terajoules of gas per day for on-site power generation, creating a domestic market for the Beetaloo resource while also potentially exporting surplus capacity to the NT grid. This is a significant commitment to gas-fired infrastructure at a scale that will operate for decades.

The proposal sits in direct tension with federal energy policy. The federal government has signalled it will require new data centre developers to match their electricity demand with new firmed renewable capacity. The NT government, by contrast, is actively promoting gas-backed power as the territory's competitive advantage for attracting data centre investment. This divergence matters for any business considering data centre location, power procurement arrangements or energy supply contracts in Australia, as it reveals that state and federal settings are not aligned.

The Beetaloo resource itself is contentious. Gas industry sources describe it as reliable baseload suitable for powering industrial loads. Climate analysis describes the resource as a potential "carbon bomb" that could release 1.4 billion tonnes of greenhouse gases and increase Australia's total emissions by up to 20 per cent if fully developed. For businesses making long-term energy procurement decisions, this underscores the need to understand not just the immediate economics of gas supply, but the carbon intensity, policy risk and stranded asset risk embedded in gas-dependent infrastructure built today.

The project remains early stage, with Beetaloo Digital seeking consortium partners and conducting feasibility studies. No final investment decision has been made. But the land commitment signals serious government backing for a gas-centred development model, which will influence how other large energy users assess their options for locating or expanding operations in the Territory.
