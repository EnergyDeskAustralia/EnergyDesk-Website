Australia lacks electric car data for grid planning

The Snapshot: Australia's energy transition planning is being hamstrung by a decade-long collapse in public data systems. Businesses making electricity procurement and grid planning decisions are working without reliable information on EV uptake, charging loads and their geographic spread, leaving grids and energy strategies vulnerable to miscalculation.

The problem runs deep. Federal science funding as a share of GDP has fallen sharply since 2014, with CSIRO alone facing up to 350 job cuts despite recent budget injections. The Australian Bureau of Statistics discontinued its Motor Vehicle Census after 2021 and dropped vehicle questions from the 2021 Census. The result is that as electric vehicles approach half of new car sales, the national data source remains VFACTS, an industry sales report designed to track showroom competition, not to inform grid planning or energy strategy.

This gap has real consequences for system planning. AEMO now formally treats EVs as a core energy system component in its 2026 Integrated System Plan, forecasting 80 per cent of vehicles on Australian roads will be electric by 2050. AEMO needs to know where battery loads concentrate, when charging peaks occur, and how vehicle-to-grid capacity can balance supply and demand. Without that data, grid operators and energy buyers cannot plan for the fact that EVs can charge when renewables are abundant and export power back when supply is tight.

The EV data gap is a symptom of a larger withdrawal from evidence-based planning. Australia's total research and development investment is now 1.66 per cent of GDP against an OECD average of 2.73 per cent, and falling in real terms. Businesses and regulators making procurement decisions on electricity, battery storage and grid infrastructure are operating with structural information deficits across the net zero transition. Without investment in public data systems, the risk is that energy infrastructure decisions will miss critical opportunities for efficiency and cost savings that better data would reveal.
