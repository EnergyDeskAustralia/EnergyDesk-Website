CSIRO backs solar and wind for cheapest energy by 2050

The Snapshot: New modelling from CSIRO and AEMO confirms solar, wind and batteries remain the lowest-cost path to net zero for Australia's electricity sector, with grid costs expected to fall further to $80–90/MWh over the next three years. For energy buyers, this reinforces that renewable-plus-storage is the most economically resilient strategy, especially as gas turbine costs rise due to global supply pressures and competition from data centre demand.

The GenCost 2025–26 report, released this week, reaffirms what the draft concluded in December: solar PV and onshore wind will deliver 93% of electricity supply in a least-cost net-zero pathway by 2050. Both technologies offer the lowest levelised cost of electricity today and are forecast to remain the cheapest option through to 2050. Battery storage has fallen in cost so sharply—deployed at utility scale and in hundreds of thousands of homes—that average generation costs are now expected to fall from $104/MWh in 2025 to $80–90/MWh within three years. For energy buyers, this trajectory means the economics of renewable procurement are strengthening, not weakening.

A new factor has entered CSIRO and AEMO's analysis: the global data centre boom is driving up costs for gas turbine equipment. Gas turbine costs have risen for four consecutive years, and new plant components now carry lead times of three to four years. This matters for Australian data centre operators considering how to power their facilities. The report notes that waiting for new gas capacity may be slower than deploying renewables-plus-storage, at least relative to the US experience where some large data centres have connected faster to gas. For any large energy consumer in Australia, this suggests electrification paired with on-site or nearby renewable generation and storage may deliver faster, cheaper project timelines than relying on new gas infrastructure.

The modelling also captures how battery storage has insulated Australia from global energy shocks. The report notes that rising Middle East tensions have had little impact on electricity prices here, partly because lower gas dependence—thanks to battery deployment—has weakened the transmission of fuel price volatility into the grid. This points to a strategic advantage for energy buyers: renewable-heavy systems with strong storage are more resilient to global commodity price swings and supply chain disruption, factors that will likely persist given ongoing geopolitical tensions and inflation pressures on capital equipment.
